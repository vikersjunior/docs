## Hydration & Nutrition

Good nutrition and consistent hydration form the foundation that every other fitness habit is built on. Neglecting either one makes training harder and recovery slower.

<Accordion title="Drink at least 8 glasses of water daily to stay hydrated">
  Water regulates your body temperature, lubricates your joints, and transports nutrients to your muscles. Aim for at least eight 250 ml glasses spread throughout the day — not just around workouts. If you're exercising in heat or sweating heavily, you'll need even more.

  <Info>
    Use the **Water Intake Calculator** in FitStart Planner to get a personalized daily target based on your weight and activity level. The formula accounts for light, moderate, and high activity multipliers so your number is more accurate than a one-size-fits-all recommendation.
  </Info>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Eat protein within 30 minutes after working out">
  Your muscles are most receptive to nutrients in the window immediately after exercise. Consuming protein within 30 minutes of finishing a session gives your body the building blocks it needs to repair muscle fibres and adapt to the training load. A simple option like Greek yogurt, a handful of nuts, or a boiled egg works perfectly — you don't need a protein shake to hit this window.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Mix cardio and strength training for best results">
  Cardio improves your heart health, burns calories, and builds endurance. Strength training builds muscle, raises your resting metabolism, and protects your joints. Doing both — even in the same week — delivers better all-round results than focusing exclusively on one. FitStart Planner's Workout Library includes routines across both categories, so you can alternate them throughout your weekly schedule.
</Accordion>

## Workout Habits

How you structure and approach each session matters as much as the sessions themselves. These habits make your time in the app (and in your living room) more effective.

<Accordion title="Start small - 10 minutes of exercise is better than none">
  When motivation is low or time is tight, the instinct is to skip entirely. Resist it. Even a ten-minute walk or a brief bodyweight circuit moves you in the right direction, builds the habit of showing up, and often turns into a longer session once you've started. Use the **Random Workout Generator** on any day you can't decide what to do — it removes the decision and gets you moving.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Warm up before every workout to prevent injury">
  A five-minute warm-up gradually raises your heart rate, increases blood flow to your muscles, and prepares your joints for the range of motion your workout requires. Skipping it might save time in the short term, but even minor soft-tissue injuries can set you back weeks. Dynamic movements like leg swings, arm circles, and light marching in place are enough to make a difference.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Consistency beats intensity - show up every day">
  A moderate workout done every day outperforms an intense workout done occasionally. Your body adapts to repeated, predictable stress over time, so showing up consistently — even on low-energy days — is more valuable than pushing hard once a week. Use the **Weekly Planner** to schedule your sessions in advance so the decision is already made before the day starts.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Track your progress to stay motivated">
  Progress is often invisible in the moment and only obvious in hindsight. Keeping a simple log — even just noting which routine you completed and how it felt — gives you concrete evidence that you're improving. Over several weeks, that record becomes one of the most effective motivational tools you have. You can use the Weekly Planner to review past scheduling patterns and pair it with a personal note or journal app for more detail.
</Accordion>

## Recovery

Recovery is not the opposite of training — it's part of training. The adaptations that make you fitter happen during rest, not during the workout itself.

<Accordion title="Get 7-9 hours of sleep for optimal recovery">
  Sleep is when your body releases growth hormone, repairs muscle tissue, and consolidates motor patterns learned during exercise. Consistently getting fewer than seven hours blunts your gains, increases injury risk, and makes workouts feel harder than they should. Treat sleep as a non-negotiable part of your fitness plan, not an afterthought.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Listen to your body and rest when needed">
  Soreness after a new workout is normal. Sharp pain, persistent joint discomfort, or exhaustion that doesn't improve after a night's sleep are signals to back off. Pushing through genuine fatigue or pain dramatically increases your injury risk. Take an unplanned rest day when your body asks for one — you'll come back to the next session stronger for it.

  <Warning>
    If you experience chest pain, dizziness, or shortness of breath during exercise, stop immediately and seek medical attention.
  </Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Don't skip rest days - recovery is crucial">
  Scheduled rest days are not wasted days. They're the sessions where your muscles rebuild, your nervous system recharges, and your motivation resets. Build at least one or two rest days into your weekly schedule using the Weekly Planner, and treat them with the same commitment you give to your active sessions.
</Accordion>

## Mindset

The mental side of building a fitness habit is just as important as the physical. These tips help you stay consistent when the initial excitement fades.

<Accordion title="Set realistic goals and celebrate small wins">
  Ambitious long-term goals are motivating, but they can feel discouraging if progress seems slow. Break your main goal into smaller milestones — completing your first full week of planned sessions, trying a new routine, or hitting your daily water target for five days straight. Each small win reinforces the habit and keeps momentum going between the bigger milestones.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Find a workout buddy for accountability">
  Committing to exercise with another person dramatically increases the likelihood that you'll follow through. A workout buddy doesn't have to be in the same room — sharing your weekly plan with a friend and checking in at the end of each week creates the same accountability effect. You're far less likely to skip a session when someone else is expecting you to show up.
</Accordion>
